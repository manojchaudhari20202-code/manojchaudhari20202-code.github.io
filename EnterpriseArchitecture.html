Business Architecture

 

Strategy & Planning

 

- **Business capability modeling** 

  A technique to represent what a business does (its capabilities) independently of how it does it (people, processes, technology). Capabilities are stable building blocks (e.g., "Order Management," "Customer Service") used to analyze gaps, plan investments, and align strategy with execution.

 

- **Value stream mapping** 

  A lean-management method for analyzing the current state of a process and designing a future state. It maps the steps, information flows, and timelines required to deliver a product or service to a customer, highlighting waste and opportunities for improvement.

 

- **Business motivation model (goals, objectives, strategies)** 

  A framework that captures the factors behind business decisions. It defines:

  - **Ends:** Vision, goals, objectives.

  - **Means:** Missions, strategies, tactics.

  - **Influencers:** Internal/external factors (e.g., regulations, competition).

  - **Assessments:** SWOT analysis. 

  It ensures traceability from high-level strategy to execution.

 

- **Operating model design** 

  Defines how a company organizes its people, processes, and technology to deliver value. Common patterns include diversification, coordination, replication, and unification (based on business process integration and standardization). It guides decisions about centralization vs. decentralization.

 

- **Business process architecture** 

  A high-level blueprint of an organization's core, supporting, and management processes. It shows how processes interrelate and align with business capabilities, often visualized as a process hierarchy or a process map.

 

- **Organization structure mapping** 

  Depicts the formal reporting relationships, roles, and responsibilities within an enterprise. Used to align organizational units with capabilities and processes, identify decision rights, and support change management.

 

- **Strategic alignment frameworks (e.g., Balanced Scorecard)** 

  Methods to ensure that business and IT strategies are consistent and mutually supportive. The Balanced Scorecard translates strategy into four perspectives: financial, customer, internal processes, and learning & growth, linking them with cause-and-effect relationships.

 

Business Processes

 

- **Process modeling techniques (BPMN, flowcharts)** 

  Visual notations to represent business processes. BPMN (Business Process Model and Notation) is a standard with symbols for events, activities, gateways, and flows, enabling both business and technical audiences to understand and automate processes.

 

- **Process maturity assessment** 

  Evaluation of how well processes are defined, managed, measured, and optimized. Models like CMMI (Capability Maturity Model Integration) provide levels from initial (ad-hoc) to optimizing, helping organizations identify improvement areas.

 

- **Process optimization and reengineering** 

  The practice of improving process efficiency, effectiveness, and agility. Optimization involves incremental changes (e.g., Lean Six Sigma), while reengineering (BPR) is a radical redesign to achieve dramatic improvements in cost, quality, or speed.

 

- **End-to-end process design** 

  Designing a complete process that spans functional silos, focusing on the customer journey and value delivery. It considers inputs, outputs, handoffs, and supporting systems to ensure seamless integration.

 

- **Process governance frameworks** 

  Structures and policies to oversee process management. They define roles (process owners, stewards), decision rights, standards, and review mechanisms to ensure processes remain aligned with business goals.

 

- **Business rules management** 

  The practice of capturing, storing, and managing business logic (rules) separately from application code. Rules define constraints, conditions, and derivations (e.g., "If order value > $1000, require manager approval"). Tools like BRMS enable agility in rule changes.

 

Stakeholder Management

 

- **Stakeholder analysis and mapping** 

  Identifying individuals or groups with an interest in a project or architecture, assessing their influence, interest, and attitudes. Tools like power/interest grids help prioritize engagement and communication strategies.

 

- **Requirements gathering techniques** 

  Methods to elicit business needs, including interviews, workshops, surveys, document analysis, observation, and prototyping. The goal is to capture functional and non-functional requirements that will guide architecture design.

 

- **Business case development** 

  A structured proposal that justifies an investment by outlining the problem, options, costs, benefits, risks, and expected outcomes. It provides a basis for decision-making and ongoing value tracking.

 

- **Cost-benefit analysis** 

  Quantitative comparison of the expected costs and benefits of a project or initiative. It includes direct/indirect costs, tangible/intangible benefits, ROI, NPV, and payback period to support investment decisions.

 

- **Change management strategies** 

  Approaches to prepare, support, and help individuals and organizations adopt change. Models like ADKAR (Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement) or Kotter's 8-step process guide communication, training, and resistance management.

 

- **Communication planning** 

  Creating a plan to ensure stakeholders receive the right information at the right time. It defines messages, channels, frequency, and owners, tailored to different stakeholder groups to foster buy-in and transparency.

 

Performance & Metrics

 

- **KPI definition and measurement** 

  Key Performance Indicators are quantifiable measures that track progress toward strategic goals. They should be SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) and aligned with business objectives.

 

- **Business metrics frameworks** 

  Structured sets of metrics that provide a holistic view of performance. Examples include the Balanced Scorecard, OKRs (Objectives and Key Results), and industry-specific metrics (e.g., customer churn, net promoter score).

 

- **ROI calculation** 

  Return on Investment measures the profitability of an investment relative to its cost. It is calculated as (Net Benefit / Cost) × 100%. Used to compare alternatives and justify expenditures.

 

- **Business value realization** 

  The process of tracking and ensuring that expected benefits from a project are actually achieved. It involves defining value drivers, measuring outcomes, and adjusting course if benefits are not materializing.

 

- **Performance dashboards** 

  Visual displays of key metrics and KPIs, often with real-time data, to provide at-a-glance insight into business performance. They support monitoring, analysis, and decision-making at various levels.

 

 

Data Architecture

 

Data Strategy & Governance

 

- **Data governance frameworks** 

  Policies, processes, and roles that ensure data is managed as an asset. Frameworks (e.g., DMBOK, COBIT) define data ownership, accountability, and decision rights to improve data quality, security, and compliance.

 

- **Data quality management** 

  The practice of ensuring data is fit for its intended use. It includes defining quality dimensions (accuracy, completeness, consistency, timeliness, uniqueness, validity), measuring them, and implementing corrective actions.

 

- **Master data management (MDM)** 

  The discipline of creating a single, consistent, authoritative view of key business entities (customer, product, supplier) across systems. MDM involves data consolidation, cleansing, and synchronization to support operational and analytical needs.

 

- **Metadata management** 

  Managing data about data – technical (schema, data types), business (definitions, ownership), and operational (lineage, run stats). It enables data discovery, understanding, and governance.

 

- **Data stewardship models** 

  Defines roles and responsibilities for data oversight. Data stewards are accountable for data quality, metadata, and policy adherence within a domain. Models vary from centralized (single team) to decentralized (embedded in business units).

 

- **Data policies and standards** 

  Formal rules governing data usage, security, privacy, and lifecycle. They ensure consistency and compliance across the organization, covering areas like data classification, access controls, retention, and sharing.

 

Data Modeling

 

- **Conceptual data modeling** 

  A high-level, business-focused representation of the main data entities and their relationships, independent of any technical implementation. It uses concepts like "Customer," "Order," "Product" to communicate with stakeholders.

 

- **Logical data modeling** 

  A detailed model that specifies entities, attributes, primary keys, and relationships, but is still independent of a specific database system. It defines the structure and rules of the data, often normalized to reduce redundancy.

 

- **Physical data modeling** 

  A model that translates the logical design into a specific database implementation, including tables, columns, indexes, partitions, and storage details. It is optimized for performance and scalability.

 

- **Dimensional modeling (star schema, snowflake)** 

  A design technique for data warehouses that organizes data into fact tables (measures, metrics) and dimension tables (descriptive attributes). Star schema has denormalized dimensions; snowflake normalizes them. It supports intuitive querying and fast aggregations.

 

- **Entity-relationship diagrams** 

  Visual representations of entities (tables) and the relationships between them, using notations like Crow's Foot or UML. They are used in both logical and physical modeling to document database design.

 

- **Data normalization techniques** 

  The process of organizing data to reduce redundancy and improve integrity. Normal forms (1NF, 2NF, 3NF, BCNF) define rules for eliminating duplicate data and ensuring dependencies are logical.

 

Data Management

 

- **Data lifecycle management** 

  The policies and processes that govern data from creation or acquisition through storage, usage, archival, and deletion. It ensures data is available when needed and disposed of when no longer required, considering cost and compliance.

 

- **Data cataloging and classification** 

  Creating an inventory of data assets (datasets, tables, reports) with metadata (tags, descriptions, sensitivity). Classification labels data based on sensitivity (public, internal, confidential) to apply appropriate controls.

 

- **Data lineage and provenance** 

  Tracking the origin, transformations, and movement of data over time. Lineage helps in impact analysis, debugging, auditing, and understanding data trustworthiness.

 

- **Reference data management** 

  Managing code sets and lookup values (e.g., country codes, statuses) that are used across systems. It ensures consistency by providing a single source of truth for these static data elements.

 

- **Data retention and archival** 

  Policies defining how long data must be kept (for legal, regulatory, or business reasons) and when it can be moved to cheaper storage (archival) or deleted. Balances accessibility with cost and compliance.

 

- **Data privacy and compliance (GDPR, CCPA)** 

  Adherence to regulations that protect personal data. This involves data minimization, consent management, right to access/erasure, pseudonymization, and ensuring that data handling practices meet legal requirements.

 

Data Integration & Quality

 

- **ETL/ELT processes** 

  Methods to extract data from source systems, transform it (cleanse, enrich, aggregate), and load it into a target (data warehouse, data lake). ETL transforms before loading; ELT loads raw data and transforms within the target (common in cloud data lakes).

 

- **Data integration patterns** 

  Common approaches to combine data from different sources: batch (scheduled loads), real-time (streaming), change data capture (CDC), data virtualization (on-demand federation), and API-based integration.

 

- **Data migration strategies** 

  Approaches to move data from one system to another, such as big bang (all at once) or trickle (phased). Includes planning, extraction, cleansing, validation, and rollback procedures to minimize disruption.

 

- **Data quality dimensions** 

  Characteristics used to measure data quality: accuracy (correctness), completeness (all required data present), consistency (no conflicts across systems), timeliness (up-to-date), uniqueness (no duplicates), and validity (conforms to rules).

 

- **Data profiling techniques** 

  Analyzing source data to understand its structure, content, and quality. Profiling reveals patterns, anomalies, nulls, and data types, helping to assess suitability for integration and identify issues.

 

- **Data cleansing approaches** 

  Methods to correct or remove inaccurate, incomplete, or irrelevant data. This may involve standardization, deduplication, validation, and enrichment using rules or machine learning.

 

Advanced Data Topics

 

- **Data warehousing architecture** 

  A system designed for reporting and analysis, typically consisting of staging area, ETL/ELT processes, central warehouse (normalized or dimensional), and data marts. Modern architectures include cloud-based solutions like Snowflake, Redshift.

 

- **Data lake design** 

  A storage repository that holds raw data in native formats until needed. It supports structured, semi-structured, and unstructured data. Key considerations: data ingestion, cataloging, governance, and processing layers (e.g., using Hadoop, AWS Lake Formation).

 

- **Big data architectures** 

  Frameworks to handle massive volumes, velocity, and variety of data. Technologies include distributed storage (HDFS, S3) and processing (Spark, Flink). Architectures often involve lambda (batch + speed layers) or kappa (stream-only) designs.

 

- **Real-time data processing** 

  Processing data as it arrives, with minimal latency. Used for fraud detection, monitoring, personalization. Technologies: Kafka, Storm, Spark Streaming, Flink. Architectures involve event streams and complex event processing.

 

- **Data analytics platforms** 

  Integrated environments for data analysis, including data preparation, visualization, and machine learning. Examples: Tableau, Power BI, Databricks, Google Analytics. They support self-service analytics and advanced analytics.

 

- **Data security and encryption** 

  Protecting data at rest, in transit, and during processing. Includes encryption (AES, TLS), tokenization, access controls (RBAC, ABAC), data masking, and auditing. Essential for compliance and risk management.

 

Application Architecture

 

Application Strategy

 

- **Application portfolio management** 

  The practice of managing the inventory of applications within an organization, assessing their business value, cost, technical health, and risks. It helps in rationalization, retirement, and investment decisions.

 

- **Application rationalization** 

  The process of analyzing the application portfolio to reduce complexity, eliminate redundancies, and align with business goals. Actions may include retire, retain, consolidate, replace, or invest.

 

- **Build vs. buy decisions** 

  Evaluating whether to develop a custom application in-house or purchase a commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) product. Factors: cost, time, strategic differentiation, customization needs, maintenance, and vendor lock-in.

 

- **Cloud vs. on-premise strategy** 

  Deciding where to deploy applications based on requirements like control, security, scalability, cost, and compliance. Hybrid approaches combine both, while cloud-native strategies leverage cloud services for agility.

 

- **Legacy application modernization** 

  The process of updating older applications to newer platforms, architectures, or languages. Approaches: rehost (lift-and-shift), replatform, refactor, rearchitect, rebuild, or replace. Goals: improve performance, maintainability, and cost-efficiency.

 

- **Application lifecycle management** 

  The governance of an application from inception to retirement. It covers requirements, design, development, testing, deployment, operations, and sunsetting. ALM tools help manage versions, releases, and traceability.

 

Architecture Patterns & Styles

 

- **Monolithic architecture** 

  A traditional model where all components of an application (UI, business logic, data access) are packaged together as a single unit. Simple to develop but can become hard to scale and maintain as the application grows.

 

- **Microservices architecture** 

  An approach where an application is composed of small, independent services that communicate over well-defined APIs. Each service is focused on a business capability and can be developed, deployed, and scaled independently.

 

- **Service-oriented architecture (SOA)** 

  An architectural style that promotes loose coupling and reusability through services. Services are coarse-grained and often communicate via an enterprise service bus (ESB). SOA emphasizes interoperability and governance.

 

- **Event-driven architecture** 

  A pattern where components react to events as they occur. Events are produced, detected, and consumed asynchronously, often via message brokers. Enables high scalability, decoupling, and real-time processing.

 

- **Layered architecture (n-tier)** 

  Organizes application into horizontal layers such as presentation, business logic, and data access. Each layer has a specific responsibility and communicates with adjacent layers. Common in traditional enterprise applications.

 

- **Serverless architecture** 

  A cloud-native pattern where applications are built using functions (FaaS) that run in stateless containers, triggered by events. The cloud provider manages infrastructure, scaling, and availability. Reduces operational overhead.

 

- **Domain-driven design** 

  A design approach that focuses on modeling complex business domains. It defines bounded contexts, entities, value objects, aggregates, and repositories to align software with business concepts.

 

Integration & Interfaces

 

- **API design and management** 

  Designing application programming interfaces that are consistent, secure, and developer-friendly. Management includes versioning, documentation, access control, rate limiting, and analytics. REST, GraphQL, and gRPC are common styles.

 

- **Enterprise service bus (ESB)** 

  A middleware tool that facilitates communication between services in SOA. It handles message routing, transformation, protocol conversion, and orchestration, acting as a central integration backbone.

 

- **Integration patterns (point-to-point, hub-and-spoke)** 

  Point-to-point connects systems directly; simple but can lead to a "spaghetti" of connections. Hub-and-spoke centralizes integration via a hub (like ESB), reducing point-to-point links but creating a single point of failure.

 

- **Middleware technologies** 

  Software that connects applications and services, enabling communication and data exchange. Examples: message queues (RabbitMQ), application servers (JBoss), integration platforms (MuleSoft, Apache Camel).

 

- **Message queuing systems** 

  Asynchronous communication using queues to decouple producers and consumers. Messages are stored until processed, providing reliability and load leveling. Examples: JMS, ActiveMQ, Kafka (which also supports streaming).

 

- **API gateway patterns** 

  A single entry point for API requests, handling routing, composition, authentication, rate limiting, and monitoring. It simplifies client interactions and can enforce cross-cutting concerns. Common in microservices.

 

Application Design Principles

 

- **SOLID principles** 

  A set of five design principles for object-oriented software: Single Responsibility, Open-Closed, Liskov Substitution, Interface Segregation, and Dependency Inversion. They promote maintainability, scalability, and testability.

 

- **Separation of concerns** 

  Designing software so that different concerns (e.g., user interface, business logic, data persistence) are separated into distinct modules or layers. This improves modularity and makes changes easier.

 

- **Coupling and cohesion** 

  Coupling measures the degree of interdependence between modules (low coupling is desirable). Cohesion measures how closely related the responsibilities of a single module are (high cohesion is desirable). Good design aims for low coupling and high cohesion.

 

- **Scalability patterns** 

  Strategies to handle increased load: horizontal scaling (adding more instances), vertical scaling (adding more power to a single instance), caching, load balancing, database sharding, and asynchronous processing.

 

- **Resilience and fault tolerance** 

  Designing applications to withstand failures. Techniques include retries, circuit breakers, timeouts, bulkheads, fallbacks, and graceful degradation. Chaos engineering tests resilience.

 

- **Performance optimization** 

  Techniques to improve response times and throughput: code profiling, caching (CDN, Redis), database indexing, query optimization, lazy loading, and minimizing network latency.

 

Development & Deployment

 

- **Software development lifecycle (SDLC)** 

  The process of planning, creating, testing, and deploying software. Models include waterfall, iterative, agile (Scrum, Kanban), and DevOps. Each phase ensures quality and alignment with requirements.

 

- **DevOps practices** 

  A culture and set of practices that combine development and operations to shorten development cycles and deliver high-quality software continuously. Includes CI/CD, infrastructure as code, monitoring, and collaboration.

 

- **CI/CD pipelines** 

  Continuous Integration (CI) automatically builds and tests code changes. Continuous Delivery/Deployment (CD) automates the release process, enabling frequent and reliable deployments. Tools: Jenkins, GitLab CI, GitHub Actions.

 

- **Containerization (Docker, Kubernetes)** 

  Containerization packages applications with their dependencies into lightweight, portable containers. Docker is a popular container runtime; Kubernetes orchestrates container deployment, scaling, and management.

 

- **Version control strategies** 

  Managing changes to code and artifacts. Git is widely used, with branching strategies like GitFlow, trunk-based development, or GitHub Flow to support collaboration and release management.

 

- **Release management** 

  The process of planning, scheduling, and controlling software releases. It ensures that releases are deployed smoothly with minimal disruption, including rollback plans, feature toggles, and environment management.

 

Application Security

 

- **Authentication and authorization** 

  Authentication verifies identity (who you are); authorization determines permissions (what you can do). Common methods: passwords, multi-factor authentication (MFA), OAuth, and role-based access control (RBAC).

 

- **OAuth, SAML, OpenID Connect** 

  Protocols for identity and access management:

  - **OAuth 2.0:** Delegated authorization (e.g., allowing an app to access your data).

  - **SAML:** XML-based authentication and authorization, common in enterprise SSO.

  - **OpenID Connect:** Identity layer on top of OAuth 2.0 for authentication.

 

- **Secure coding practices** 

  Guidelines to prevent vulnerabilities like SQL injection, XSS, CSRF, and buffer overflows. Includes input validation, output encoding, least privilege, and using security libraries.

 

- **Application vulnerability assessment** 

  Automated or manual scanning to identify security weaknesses in applications. Tools: SAST (static analysis), DAST (dynamic analysis), and penetration testing.

 

- **Security testing approaches** 

  Techniques to ensure security: threat modeling, security unit tests, fuzzing, and security regression testing. Integrated into CI/CD pipelines (DevSecOps).

 

- **Identity and access management** 

  The framework for managing digital identities and controlling access to resources. It includes user provisioning, SSO, directory services (LDAP, Active Directory), and access governance.

 

Technology Architecture

 

Infrastructure Architecture

 

- **Data center design** 

  Planning physical facilities to house servers, storage, and networking equipment. Considerations: power, cooling, redundancy (N+1, 2N), cabling, security, and space optimization. Modern designs include modular and hyperscale data centers.

 

- **Network architecture** 

  The design of a network, including topology (LAN, WAN), protocols, devices (routers, switches, firewalls), and addressing. It defines how data flows and ensures connectivity, performance, and security.

 

- **Server architectures** 

  The design of server hardware and software, including form factors (rack, blade, tower), CPU, memory, and storage. Virtualization and containerization abstract physical resources.

 

- **Storage architecture (SAN, NAS, object storage)** 

  - **SAN (Storage Area Network):** Block-level storage accessed over high-speed network, used for databases.

  - **NAS (Network Attached Storage):** File-level storage over network, simpler sharing.

  - **Object storage:** Stores data as objects with metadata, highly scalable (e.g., S3, Swift).

 

- **Virtualization technologies** 

  Abstracting physical hardware to run multiple virtual machines (VMs) on a single host. Hypervisors (VMware, KVM) manage VMs, enabling better resource utilization and isolation.

 

- **High availability and disaster recovery** 

  Strategies to ensure service continuity: failover clustering, redundant components, data replication, backup/restore, and geographically distributed sites. RPO (Recovery Point Objective) and RTO (Recovery Time Objective) define acceptable data loss and downtime.

 

Cloud Architecture

 

- **Cloud service models (IaaS, PaaS, SaaS)** 

  - **IaaS (Infrastructure as a Service):** Virtualized computing resources (VMs, storage, networks) – e.g., AWS EC2.

  - **PaaS (Platform as a Service):** Platform for developing and deploying apps without managing underlying infrastructure – e.g., Heroku, Google App Engine.

  - **SaaS (Software as a Service):** Fully managed software applications delivered over the internet – e.g., Salesforce, Office 365.

 

- **Cloud deployment models (public, private, hybrid, multi-cloud)** 

  - **Public:** Services offered over public internet, shared infrastructure.

  - **Private:** Dedicated to a single organization (on-premises or hosted).

  - **Hybrid:** Mix of public and private, with orchestration.

  - **Multi-cloud:** Using multiple public cloud providers to avoid vendor lock-in or leverage best-of-breed.

 

- **Cloud migration strategies** 

  Approaches to move workloads to cloud: rehost (lift-and-shift), replatform (lift, tweak, shift), refactor/rearchitect (modify for cloud-native), repurchase (move to SaaS), retire, retain.

 

- **Cloud-native architecture** 

  Designing applications specifically for cloud environments, leveraging microservices, containers, serverless, and managed services. It emphasizes scalability, resilience, and agility.

 

- **Cloud cost optimization** 

  Practices to manage and reduce cloud spending: right-sizing instances, using reserved/spot instances, auto-scaling, monitoring usage, and tagging resources for chargeback.

 

- **Cloud security best practices** 

  Shared responsibility model: provider secures infrastructure, customer secures data and configurations. Practices include identity and access management (IAM), encryption, network security (VPC, firewalls), and compliance monitoring.

 

Enterprise Technology Platforms

 

- **Operating systems selection** 

  Choosing OS based on application requirements, support, security, and cost. Common: Windows Server, Linux distributions (RHEL, Ubuntu), and Unix variants. Containerization reduces OS dependency.

 

- **Database management systems** 

  Software to store, manage, and query data. Types: relational (Oracle, MySQL, PostgreSQL), NoSQL (MongoDB, Cassandra), NewSQL, and in-memory (Redis). Selection based on data model, scalability, consistency, and performance.

 

- **Middleware platforms** 

  Software that connects applications and services, providing messaging, integration, and application server capabilities. Examples: WebSphere, JBoss, TIBCO, MuleSoft.

 

- **Enterprise content management** 

  Systems for managing documents, records, and digital assets. ECM includes capture, storage, workflow, collaboration, and compliance features. Examples: SharePoint, Alfresco, OpenText.

 

- **Collaboration platforms** 

  Tools that enable communication and teamwork: email (Exchange), messaging (Slack, Teams), video conferencing (Zoom), and project management (Jira, Trello).

 

- **Monitoring and management tools** 

  Solutions for observing infrastructure and applications: performance monitoring (Nagios, Prometheus), log management (Splunk, ELK stack), and APM (AppDynamics, Dynatrace).

 

Network & Connectivity

 

- **Network topologies** 

  Physical or logical arrangement of network nodes: star, bus, ring, mesh, tree. Topology affects performance, fault tolerance, and cost.

 

- **Network protocols (TCP/IP, DNS, DHCP)** 

  - **TCP/IP:** Core protocols for internet communication, ensuring reliable data transmission.

  - **DNS:** Translates domain names to IP addresses.

  - **DHCP:** Automatically assigns IP addresses to devices.

 

- **Load balancing strategies** 

  Distributing traffic across multiple servers to improve performance and availability. Algorithms: round-robin, least connections, IP hash. Can be implemented via hardware (F5) or software (NGINX, HAProxy).

 

- **Content delivery networks (CDN)** 

  Geographically distributed servers that cache content (images, videos, web pages) closer to users, reducing latency and offloading origin servers. Examples: Akamai, Cloudflare, AWS CloudFront.

 

- **VPN and remote access** 

  Virtual Private Networks create secure encrypted tunnels over public networks for remote users to access corporate resources. Protocols: IPsec, SSL/TLS.

 

- **Software-defined networking (SDN)** 

  An approach that decouples network control (control plane) from forwarding (data plane), enabling centralized management and programmability. Increases agility and automation.

 

Security Architecture

 

- **Defense in depth** 

  A layered security strategy that uses multiple controls (physical, technical, administrative) so that if one fails, others still protect. Layers include perimeter, network, host, application, and data.

 

- **Zero trust architecture** 

  A security model that assumes no implicit trust, requiring continuous verification for every access request, regardless of origin. Principles: never trust, always verify; least privilege; micro-segmentation.

 

- **Firewall strategies** 

  Firewalls filter traffic based on rules. Types: packet-filtering, stateful inspection, next-generation firewalls (with application awareness, IPS). Deployment: network perimeter, host-based, internal segmentation.

 

- **Intrusion detection/prevention systems** 

  IDS monitors network traffic for suspicious activity and alerts; IPS takes action to block it. Signatures and anomaly-based detection are used.

 

- **Security information and event management (SIEM)** 

  Solutions that aggregate and analyze security logs from various sources to detect, investigate, and respond to threats in real time. Examples: Splunk ES, IBM QRadar.

 

- **Encryption technologies** 

  Protecting data confidentiality via algorithms: symmetric (AES) for bulk encryption, asymmetric (RSA) for key exchange. Use cases: data at rest (disk/file encryption), data in transit (TLS/SSL), and data in use (homomorphic encryption – emerging).

 

- **PKI and certificate management** 

  Public Key Infrastructure manages digital certificates and public-key encryption. It includes Certificate Authorities (CAs), registration authorities, and certificate lifecycle management (issuance, renewal, revocation).

 

Emerging Technologies

 

- **Internet of Things (IoT) architecture** 

  Framework for connected devices: edge devices, gateways, cloud platforms, and applications. Key considerations: connectivity protocols (MQTT, CoAP), data ingestion, device management, and security.

 

- **Edge computing** 

  Processing data near the source (devices, sensors) instead of centralized cloud, reducing latency and bandwidth. Used in IoT, autonomous vehicles, real-time analytics.

 

- **Artificial Intelligence/Machine Learning infrastructure** 

  Hardware and software to support AI/ML workloads: GPUs/TPUs, ML frameworks (TensorFlow, PyTorch), model training and serving platforms (SageMaker, MLflow), and data pipelines.

 

- **Blockchain architecture** 

  Distributed ledger technology where transactions are recorded in blocks and linked cryptographically. Types: public (Bitcoin), private (Hyperledger), consortium. Uses: supply chain, finance, identity.

 

- **Quantum computing readiness** 

  Preparing for the potential impact of quantum computers on cryptography and computation. Organizations monitor advancements and consider post-quantum cryptography.

 

- **5G network implications** 

  5G offers high speed, low latency, and massive device connectivity. Implications for architecture: enhanced mobile broadband, ultra-reliable low-latency communications (e.g., autonomous driving), and massive IoT.

 

Cross-Domain Topics

 

Enterprise Architecture Frameworks

 

- **TOGAF (The Open Group Architecture Framework)** 

  A comprehensive framework for enterprise architecture, providing a detailed method (Architecture Development Method – ADM) and supporting artifacts. ADM phases cover vision, business, data, application, technology, and governance.

 

- **Zachman Framework** 

  A classification schema (2D matrix) that organizes architectural artifacts by interrogatives (what, how, where, who, when, why) and reification levels (contextual to detailed). It is a taxonomy, not a process.

 

- **Federal Enterprise Architecture Framework (FEAF)** 

  Developed by the U.S. government, it provides a common approach for IT management across agencies. It includes reference models (performance, business, data, application, infrastructure) and a collaborative methodology.

 

- **Gartner Enterprise Architecture Framework** 

  Focuses on business outcomes and strategy, rather than detailed artifacts. It emphasizes EA as a continuous process that drives change and supports decision-making, with a focus on future state vision.

 

- **ArchiMate modeling language** 

  An open and independent modeling language for enterprise architecture, providing a graphical notation to describe, analyze, and communicate architectures across business, data, application, and technology layers.

 

EA Governance & Standards

 

- **Architecture review boards** 

  A governing body that reviews and approves architecture decisions, standards, and roadmaps. Ensures alignment with strategy, compliance, and risk management. Members include architects, business leaders, and IT management.

 

- **Architecture principles development** 

  Creating high-level guidelines that shape architecture decisions. Principles are enduring, actionable, and align with business values (e.g., "Data is an asset," "Buy before build"). They provide a basis for evaluation.

 

- **Standards and guidelines creation** 

  Establishing technical and process standards (e.g., programming languages, platforms, documentation) to ensure consistency, interoperability, and quality. Guidelines offer recommended practices.

 

- **Compliance management** 

  Monitoring and enforcing adherence to architecture standards, policies, and regulatory requirements. It involves audits, reviews, and remediation plans to manage architecture debt.

 

- **Architecture debt management** 

  Analogous to technical debt, architecture debt refers to the cost of rework due to suboptimal architectural decisions. It accumulates over time and must be measured, tracked, and reduced through refactoring.

 

- **Technology radar and evaluation** 

  A process to monitor emerging technologies and assess their potential impact. Inspired by ThoughtWorks' Technology Radar, it categorizes technologies as adopt, trial, assess, or hold, guiding innovation.

 

Modeling & Documentation

 

- **Architecture modeling tools** 

  Software used to create and manage architectural models. They support diagramming, repository, collaboration, and analysis. Examples: Sparx EA, Ardoq, Bizzdesign, and open-source tools like Archi.

 

- **Architecture repository management** 

  A centralized database storing architectural artifacts, models, principles, and standards. It enables version control, traceability, and stakeholder access. Often part of an EA tool.

 

- **Viewpoints and views (4+1, Kruchten)** 

  A method to represent architecture from different perspectives: logical, process, development, physical, and scenarios (use cases). Each viewpoint addresses specific stakeholder concerns.

 

- **Architecture decision records (ADRs)** 

  Short documents that capture an architectural decision, its context, alternatives, and consequences. They provide a historical record and rationale, aiding communication and future understanding.

 

- **Diagramming standards (UML, C4)** 

  - **UML (Unified Modeling Language):** Standard for software modeling, including class, sequence, activity, and component diagrams.

  - **C4 model:** A hierarchical approach to visualizing software architecture: Context, Container, Component, and Code diagrams, simplifying communication.

 

Enterprise Architecture Processes

 

- **Current state assessment** 

  Analyzing the existing business, data, application, and technology landscapes. It identifies strengths, weaknesses, pain points, and dependencies, providing a baseline for improvement.

 

- **Future state visioning** 

  Defining a target architecture that aligns with business strategy and addresses current gaps. It describes desired capabilities, outcomes, and high-level design.

 

- **Gap analysis** 

  Comparing current and future states to identify what needs to change. Gaps can be in capabilities, processes, systems, or skills. They form the basis for roadmap initiatives.

 

- **Roadmap development** 

  Creating a phased plan to transition from current to future state. It includes projects, timelines, dependencies, and resource estimates. Roadmaps help prioritize and communicate change.

 

- **Impact analysis** 

  Assessing the effects of a proposed change on existing systems, processes, and stakeholders. It helps identify risks, costs, and mitigation strategies before implementation.

 

- **Transition planning** 

  Detailed planning for moving from one state to another, including migration steps, cutover strategies, training, and communication. Ensures smooth adoption and minimal disruption.

 

Soft Skills & Leadership

 

- **Influencing without authority** 

  The ability to persuade stakeholders and drive decisions without formal power. Techniques include building trust, understanding motivations, using data, and framing arguments in terms of business value.

 

- **Presenting to executives** 

  Crafting concise, impactful presentations that focus on outcomes, risks, and recommendations. Tailoring content to the audience, using visuals, and anticipating questions are key.

 

- **Conflict resolution** 

  Managing disagreements among stakeholders with different priorities. Skills include active listening, finding common ground, facilitating trade-offs, and maintaining a neutral stance.

 

- **Vendor management** 

  Selecting, contracting, and overseeing vendors to ensure they deliver value. Involves defining requirements, evaluating proposals, negotiating contracts, and monitoring performance.

 

- **Team leadership** 

  Guiding and motivating architecture teams. Includes setting vision, delegating, mentoring, fostering collaboration, and managing performance.

 

- **Continuous learning strategies** 

  Staying current in a fast-evolving field. Practices include reading, attending conferences, obtaining certifications (e.g., TOGAF, AWS Certified Solutions Architect), participating in communities, and hands-on experimentation.
